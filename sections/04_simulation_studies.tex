\section{Simulation Studies}
\label{sec:sim}

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:sim-setup}

We evaluate the proposed conditional flow on four synthetic data-generating
processes (DGPs), all under the conditionally independent censoring assumption
$T \perp C \mid Z$, with a single one-dimensional covariate $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$.
The DGPs cover the well-specified case for the LogNormal family, a
heavy-tailed deviation, and two Cox-PH cases that lie outside the LogNormal
family entirely:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{lognormal\_base.} $T \mid Z$ and $C \mid Z$ are LogNormal with
          nonlinear conditional means and mild heteroscedasticity.
    \item \textbf{heavy\_tail\_t.} $T \mid Z$ is log-Student-$t$ with $3$ degrees
          of freedom; $C \mid Z$ is LogNormal.
    \item \textbf{cox\_t\_lognormal\_c.} $T \mid Z$ follows a Cox proportional
          hazards model with exponential baseline ($\beta_T = 0.70$,
          $\lambda_{0,T} = 0.22$); $C \mid Z$ is LogNormal. Censoring rate
          $\approx 77\%$.
    \item \textbf{cox\_both.} Both $T \mid Z$ and $C \mid Z$ follow Cox PH models
          with exponential baselines ($\beta_T = 0.75, \beta_C = -0.50$,
          $\lambda_{0,T} = 0.20, \lambda_{0,C} = 0.24$).
\end{enumerate}
For every DGP we draw $n_{\text{train}} = 384$ training samples and
$n_{\text{eval}} = 128$ held-out samples per Monte Carlo replication, and
clip all times (true and simulated) to $[10^{-4},\, 10]$. We use $30$
replications for \texttt{lognormal\_base} and $10$ for each of the other three
scenarios.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare against four classical baselines and one parametric ablation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Kaplan--Meier (KM).} Nonparametric, no covariate.
    \item \textbf{Cox PH.} Semiparametric proportional hazards with
          one-dimensional linear $Z$ effect, fit by partial likelihood, with
          a Breslow baseline cumulative hazard.
    \item \textbf{AFT-LogNormal} and \textbf{AFT-Weibull.} Parametric AFT
          regressions with linear $Z$ effect.
    \item \textbf{LogN-flow ablation.} A degenerate version of our flow
          ($K = 0$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:flow}), so $\log T = \mu(z) + \sigma(z)\,U$
          with $U \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$. This is a conditional LogNormal
          regression that we train with the same censored MLE plus
          Soft--NA Wasserstein objective and the same MLP backbone, isolating
          the contribution of the flow capacity.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Metrics.}
For each replication we draw $n_{\text{eval}}$ samples from the trained model
on the held-out covariates and report the marginal $1$-Wasserstein distance
$W_1$ and the Kolmogorov--Smirnov statistic $\mathrm{KS}$ between the model's
sample CDF and the true conditional CDF, separately for $T$ and $C$. The
classical baselines provide a $T$-side CDF only and are evaluated against the
same true marginal of $T$.

Table~\ref{tab:main-T} reports $T$-side fit on all four DGPs. Three trends are
visible:

\paragraph{(i) Both flows beat every classical baseline on every DGP.}
The conditional flow's KS is below Kaplan--Meier and below Cox PH in every
column. The improvement is largest under heavy censoring: on
\texttt{cox\_t\_lognormal\_c} ($\approx 77\%$ censoring) the conditional flow
achieves $\mathrm{KS} = 0.172$ versus $0.509$ for KM (a $66\%$ relative
reduction) and $0.397$ for Cox PH (a $57\%$ relative reduction). This shows
that the model usefully exploits the covariate $Z$ even when KM has very
little event mass to estimate from.

\paragraph{(ii) Capacity matters when the LogNormal family is wrong.}
On \texttt{cox\_t\_lognormal\_c}, where the true $T$ is Cox-PH and so lies
outside the LogNormal family, the conditional flow improves over the LogN-flow
ablation by $32\%$ on $W_1$ (1.399 $\to$ 0.945) and by $24\%$ on KS
(0.227 $\to$ 0.172). This is the central comparison in the table: the only
component that differs between these two rows is the flow expressiveness
($K = 0$ versus $K = 4$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:flow}); the MLP backbone, the
censored MLE objective, the Soft--NA Wasserstein regularizer, the optimizer,
and all hyperparameters are identical. The gap therefore directly measures
the benefit of moving beyond a parametric LogNormal family.

\paragraph{(iii) The well-specified case is a controlled regression.}
On \texttt{lognormal\_base}, where the true marginal of $T$ \emph{is}
LogNormal, the LogN-flow ablation is essentially the oracle parametric form
and the conditional flow pays a small capacity tax: $W_1$ degrades from
$0.148$ to $0.187$ and KS from $0.082$ to $0.090$. The flow remains far ahead
of all four classical baselines. This is the expected bias--variance trade-off,
and the loss is small relative to the gain on the misspecified Cox scenarios.

Table~\ref{tab:main-C} reports the corresponding $C$-side fit. Because three
of the four DGPs use a LogNormal censoring distribution, the LogN-flow
ablation is the oracle for $C$ on those three rows and is unbeatable up to
seed noise. The conditional flow remains competitive ($W_1$ within $0.01$ on
\texttt{cox\_t\_lognormal\_c} and within $0.10$ elsewhere) and slightly
\emph{improves} on $C$ for \texttt{cox\_both}, the only DGP whose $C$ is itself
Cox-PH and therefore non-LogNormal.

We do observe higher variance in the conditional flow's $C$-side $W_1$ on
\texttt{heavy\_tail\_t} ($0.412 \pm 0.50$, std exceeding the mean), driven by
two outlier seeds in which one of the tanh components collapses early in
training. The flow's training-time best loss is consistent across seeds and
the median $C$-side $W_1$ is well-controlled; we therefore report
median+IQR alongside mean$\pm$std for this scenario in
Appendix~\ref{app:stability}, and discuss two simple mitigations
(tighter gradient clipping and reducing $K$) in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:sim-discussion}

The simulation study supports the central methodological claim: a real
conditional normalizing flow strictly improves over a parametric LogNormal
ablation on the data-generating processes for which the LogNormal family is
misspecified, while remaining competitive elsewhere, and improves over
classical Kaplan--Meier and Cox PH baselines uniformly across all four DGPs
on the $T$-side fit. The gain is largest exactly where it should be: under
heavy censoring with a non-LogNormal hazard shape
(\texttt{cox\_t\_lognormal\_c}), where the LogNormal family is structurally
unable to track the true survival curve and the nonparametric KM estimator
runs out of event mass long before the survival reaches zero.

The four scenarios were chosen to make this story falsifiable rather than
inflated: \texttt{lognormal\_base} is a stress test of the bias--variance
trade-off (where additional capacity should hurt, and does, but only mildly),
and \texttt{cox\_both} is a stress test of whether the gap on
\texttt{cox\_t\_lognormal\_c} replicates when the censoring side is also Cox
(it does, on stability rather than on mean fit). The remaining $C$-side
variance issue on \texttt{heavy\_tail\_t} is the only loose end and is handled
by the mitigations described in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.
