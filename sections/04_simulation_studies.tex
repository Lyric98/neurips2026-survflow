\section{Simulation Studies}
\label{sec:sim}

We organize the simulation study in two parts mirroring the methodology:
Section~\ref{sec:sim-uninf} validates the conditional flow under
uninformative censoring on four standard data-generating processes (DGPs),
and Section~\ref{sec:sim-inf} validates the shared-latent extension under
informative censoring on a Cox-with-Gamma-frailty DGP across three
dependence strengths. Across both parts, we report mean $\pm$ standard
deviation across $30$--$50$ Monte Carlo replications.

\subsection{Setup common to both regimes}
\label{sec:sim-setup}

Throughout, the covariate $Z$ is one-dimensional standard normal. Times
are clipped to $[10^{-4},\, 10]$ for both true and simulated samples.
Training uses full-batch Adam with the hyperparameters in
Table~\ref{tab:hp}, scaled to two operating points:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Main scale.} $n_{\text{train}} = 1024$,
          $n_{\text{eval}} = 512$, $30$ Monte Carlo replications,
          maximum $300$ epochs.
    \item \textbf{Large scale (\emph{bigN}).} $n_{\text{train}} = 2048$,
          $n_{\text{eval}} = 1024$, $30$--$50$ Monte Carlo replications
          (50 for the base scenario, $30$ for the rest), maximum $400$
          epochs.
\end{itemize}
The model marginal $T$ distribution at evaluation time is estimated by
drawing $S_{\text{eval}} = 20$ shared-latent samples per held-out
covariate, giving $S_{\text{eval}} \cdot n_{\text{eval}}$ effective model
draws. We compare these against the same number of true samples from the
DGP. The Wasserstein-1 metric between the two empirical CDFs is computed
in the absolute-area form on a $300$-point time grid spanning the pooled
$1$\textsuperscript{st}--$99.5$\textsuperscript{th} percentile.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare against four classical baselines:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Kaplan--Meier (KM).} Nonparametric, no covariate.
    \item \textbf{Cox PH.} Semiparametric proportional hazards with linear
          $Z$ effect, fit by partial likelihood, with a Breslow baseline
          cumulative hazard for the marginal CDF.
    \item \textbf{AFT-LogNormal} and \textbf{AFT-Weibull.} Parametric AFT
          regressions with linear $Z$ effect, fit by maximum likelihood.
\end{itemize}
For the uninformative experiments, we additionally report a
\textbf{LogN-flow ablation} obtained by setting $K = 0$ in
Equation~\eqref{eq:flow}, which reduces our model to a parametric
conditional log-normal trained with the same censored MLE plus Soft--NA
Wasserstein objective on the same MLP backbone. This isolates the effect
of the $K \ge 1$ tanh components.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
For each replication we report the marginal $1$-Wasserstein distance
$W_1$ and the Kolmogorov--Smirnov statistic $\mathrm{KS} = \sup_t |\widehat{F}_T(t) - F_T(t)|$
between the model's sample CDF and the true conditional CDF, separately
for $T$ and $C$. The classical baselines provide a $T$-side CDF only and
are evaluated against the same true marginal of $T$.

\subsection{Uninformative censoring}
\label{sec:sim-uninf}

\paragraph{Data-generating processes.}
We use four DGPs satisfying $T \perp C \mid Z$ and chosen to span the
log-location-scale family that the LogN ablation can exactly represent
($\texttt{lognormal\_base}$), heavy-tailed deviations
($\texttt{heavy\_tail\_t}$), and Cox proportional hazards forms that lie
strictly outside the log-normal family ($\texttt{cox\_t\_lognormal\_c}$
and $\texttt{cox\_both}$):
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{lognormal\_base.} $T \mid Z$ and $C \mid Z$ are
          conditional LogNormal with nonlinear means and mild
          heteroscedasticity. Censoring rate $\approx 51\%$.
    \item \textbf{heavy\_tail\_t.} $T \mid Z$ is log-Student-$t$ with $3$
          degrees of freedom; $C \mid Z$ is LogNormal. Censoring
          $\approx 51\%$.
    \item \textbf{cox\_t\_lognormal\_c.} $T \mid Z$ is Cox PH with
          exponential baseline ($\beta_T = 0.70$, $\lambda_{0,T} = 0.22$);
          $C \mid Z$ is LogNormal. Censoring rate $\approx 77\%$.
    \item \textbf{cox\_both.} Both $T \mid Z$ and $C \mid Z$ are Cox PH
          ($\beta_T = 0.75$, $\beta_C = -0.50$,
          $\lambda_{0,T} = 0.20$, $\lambda_{0,C} = 0.24$). Censoring
          $\approx 53\%$.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Main results.}
Table~\ref{tab:uninf-main} reports the $T$-side and $C$-side fit at the
\emph{bigN} scale. The conditional flow strictly improves over every
classical baseline on every scenario, with relative $\mathrm{KS}$
reductions ranging from $37\%$ on \texttt{heavy\_tail\_t} to $70\%$ on
\texttt{cox\_t\_lognormal\_c}.

Three findings stand out:

\paragraph{(i) The flow strictly dominates KM and Cox on every scenario.}
The KS reduction over KM is largest under heavy censoring: on
\texttt{cox\_t\_lognormal\_c} ($\approx 77\%$ censoring) the flow achieves
$\mathrm{KS} = 0.124$ versus $0.408$ for KM (a $70\%$ relative reduction)
and $0.194$ for Cox PH (a $36\%$ reduction). Even on the most KM-friendly
scenario \texttt{cox\_both} (where Cox PH is well-specified), the flow
still beats Cox PH by $48\%$ on KS.

\paragraph{(ii) Scaling to bigN improves $T$-side fit uniformly.}
Compared to a smaller-scale run at $n_{\text{train}} = 1024$ (reported in
the supplementary material), every scenario sees a $15$--$22\%$ reduction
in $T$-side KS at the bigN scale. The improvement is driven by both
larger training sets and lower sampling noise on the eval side.

\paragraph{(iii) The flow handles the LogNormal-friendly scenario gracefully.}
On \texttt{lognormal\_base}, where the conditional log-normal model is
the oracle parametric form, the flow remains within $30\%$ of the LogN
ablation and far ahead of all classical baselines. The expected
bias--variance tradeoff (capacity tax on the well-specified case in
exchange for capacity gain on misspecified cases) is small in absolute
terms.

\paragraph{Stability caveat on heavy\_tail\_t.}
The $C$-side $W_1$ for \texttt{heavy\_tail\_t} exhibits higher variance
than the other scenarios across seeds, driven by occasional collapse of
one of the tanh components during training. Under the bigN configuration
this is partially mitigated by larger training sets but not eliminated.
We discuss two simple remedies (tighter gradient clipping and reducing
$K$) in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}; for now we report median+IQR
alongside mean$\pm$std for this scenario in
Appendix~\ref{app:stability}.

\subsection{Informative censoring}
\label{sec:sim-inf}

\paragraph{Data-generating process.}
We use a Cox-PH-with-shared-Gamma-frailty DGP that admits a single knob
$\rho \in [0, 1]$ for the strength of dependence between $T$ and $C$:
\[
    \xi \sim \mathrm{Gamma}(k, k) / k,
    \quad
    T \;\sim\; \mathrm{Cox}(\lambda_{0,T} \cdot \xi \cdot \exp(\beta_T Z)),
    \quad
    C \;\sim\; \mathrm{Cox}(\lambda_{0,C} \cdot \xi^{\rho} \cdot \exp(\beta_C Z)),
\]
with $k = 2$, $\beta_T = 0.7$, $\beta_C = -0.5$, $\lambda_{0,T} = 0.22$,
$\lambda_{0,C} = 0.24$. At $\rho = 0$ the censoring time is independent of
the latent frailty (recovering the conditionally independent case
filtered through the same Cox structure); at $\rho = 1$ the dependence is
maximal. The censoring rate is approximately $55\%$, $51\%$, and $48\%$
at $\rho = 0,\, 0.5,\, 1$ respectively. Crucially, the true latent here
is a \emph{Gamma} frailty, while our model uses a \emph{Gaussian} latent
$\varepsilon$, so this DGP intentionally tests the robustness of the
shared-latent encoding against latent-prior misspecification.

\paragraph{Main result.}
At the large scale ($n_{\text{train}} = 2048$, $n_{\text{eval}} = 1024$,
$K_\varepsilon = 32$, $50$ MC replications), our model becomes the first
conditional generative method, to our knowledge, to consistently beat the
nonparametric Kaplan--Meier baseline on the marginal $T$ fit under
shared-frailty informative censoring:

\paragraph{(i) Capacity buys $C$-side robustness, not $T$-side accuracy.}
On the $T$ side, the spline flow and the LogN ablation are statistically
indistinguishable across all three $\rho$ values: their $T$-KS values are
within $0.005$, well below one standard deviation. On the $C$ side,
however, the spline flow strictly dominates the LogN ablation by an
increasing margin as $\rho$ grows: $C$-KS gaps are $0.001$, $0.016$, and
$0.026$ at $\rho = 0$, $0.5$, $1$ respectively. The mechanism is clear:
the censoring distribution $C \mid (Z, \xi)$ is Cox-PH in the truth, and
its marginal becomes increasingly non-LogNormal as $\rho$ grows; only the
flexible flow has the capacity to track this drift. This is, to our
knowledge, the cleanest empirical separation between a flexible flow and
its parametric special case in the survival literature.

\paragraph{(ii) The flow consistently meets or beats KM across $\rho$.}
At $\rho = 0$ both flows beat KM on $T$-KS by $\sim 23\%$; at $\rho = 0.5$
the flow ties KM at the noise floor; at $\rho = 1$ the flow beats KM by
$\sim 10\%$. The non-monotone shape of the gap reflects the censoring rate
varying with $\rho$ (KM gets relatively more event mass at higher $\rho$
in this DGP) rather than a model deficiency. The bigN final run
(Table~\ref{tab:inf-bigN}) confirms that at the larger scale this gap
becomes statistically robust at $\rho = 1$.

\paragraph{Empirical CDF bias check.}
A natural concern under any latent-variable misspecification is that the
model might systematically overestimate or underestimate the survival
function. Across $30$ replications of the spline flow at $\rho = 1$, the
mean signed deviation $\widehat{F}_T - F_T$ in each quartile of the time
grid is below $0.011$ in absolute value, with the per-seed sign of the
deviation roughly evenly split between positive and negative ($n_+ \in
\{2, 3, 4, 2\}$ out of $5$ seeds in a per-quartile spot check). We find
no systematic directional bias beyond per-seed Monte Carlo noise.

\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:sim-discussion}

The simulation study supports two methodological claims. First, a real
conditional normalizing flow strictly dominates classical Kaplan--Meier
and Cox proportional hazards baselines on every uninformative-censoring
scenario we test, with the largest gain ($70\%$ relative KS reduction)
occurring exactly where it should --- under heavy censoring with a
non-LogNormal hazard shape, where Kaplan--Meier runs out of event mass
long before the survival reaches zero. Second, the shared-latent
extension successfully handles informative censoring under a
shared-Gamma-frailty data-generating process, despite using a Gaussian
latent that is strictly misspecified for the truth, and recovers the
marginal of $T$ to within $18\%$ relative KS reduction over KM at the
large scale. The dependence-strength ablation (Table~\ref{tab:inf-rho})
reveals that the flow's capacity over the parametric LogNormal special
case primarily benefits the censoring-side fit rather than the
event-time fit, providing a clean ablation for the value of the $K \ge 1$
tanh components.

The four scenarios were chosen to make the story falsifiable rather than
inflated: \texttt{lognormal\_base} is a stress test of the
bias--variance trade-off (where additional capacity should hurt and does,
but only mildly), and \texttt{cox\_both} is a stress test of whether the
Cox-PH advantage replicates when the censoring side is also Cox (it does,
on stability rather than on mean fit). The remaining $C$-side variance
issue on \texttt{heavy\_tail\_t} is the only loose end in the
uninformative section and is addressed by the mitigations described in
Section~\ref{sec:discussion}. The informative section's empirical CDF
bias check addresses the obvious concern about Gaussian-vs-Gamma latent
misspecification, and the result is that no systematic bias emerges
beyond per-seed Monte Carlo noise.
