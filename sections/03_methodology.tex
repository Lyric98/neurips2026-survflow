\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem setup}
\label{sec:setup}

Let $T \in \mathbb{R}_{>0}$ be a failure time and $C \in \mathbb{R}_{>0}$ be
an independent right-censoring time, both depending on a covariate
$Z \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$. The observed data are i.i.d.\ tuples
\[
    \mathcal{D} = \{(X_i,\, \delta_i,\, Z_i)\}_{i=1}^{n},
    \qquad
    X_i = \min(T_i, C_i),
    \quad
    \delta_i = \mathbf{1}\{T_i \le C_i\}.
\]
Throughout this paper we assume \emph{conditionally independent
censoring}, $T \perp C \mid Z$ (the uninformative case); the extension to
shared-frailty informative censoring is sketched in
Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

Our goal is to learn generative models $g_1, g_2$ such that
\[
    T = g_1(Z, \tau_1),
    \qquad
    C = g_2(Z, \tau_2),
    \qquad
    \tau_1, \tau_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)\text{ independent},
\]
and such that the implied conditional densities $f_{T|Z}, f_{C|Z}$ and
survival functions $S_{T|Z}, S_{C|Z}$ admit closed forms suitable for the
censored log-likelihood.

\subsection{Conditional monotone normalizing flow on log-time}
\label{sec:flow}

We model both $T \mid Z$ and $C \mid Z$ with a conditional normalizing flow
on $\log T$, with a standard-Gaussian base. Concretely, define a forward map
\begin{equation}
    h_\theta(\log t;\, z)
    \;=\; a(z)\,\log t \;+\; b(z)
        \;+\; \sum_{k=1}^{K} \alpha_k(z)\, \tanh\!\left(\frac{\log t - \mu_k(z)}{s_k(z)}\right),
    \label{eq:flow}
\end{equation}
where the coefficient functions $a, b, \{\alpha_k, \mu_k, s_k\}_{k=1}^{K}$
are output by a small MLP applied to $z$. We enforce the positivity
constraints
\[
    a(z) > 0,
    \qquad
    \alpha_k(z) \ge 0,
    \qquad
    s_k(z) > 0
\]
via shifted softplus activations. Each $\tanh$ component is monotone in
$\log t$, so under these constraints $h_\theta(\cdot; z)$ is strictly
increasing in $\log t$ for every $z$, hence invertible. We take the base
distribution to be standard normal,
\(
    U \;=\; h_\theta(\log T;\, Z) \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}(0, 1).
\)

\paragraph{Closed-form density and survival.}
By construction $F_{T|Z}(t \mid z) = \Phi\!\left(h_\theta(\log t;\, z)\right)$,
which yields, by the chain rule,
\begin{align}
    \log f_{T|Z}(t \mid z)
    &\;=\;
    \log \phi\!\left(h_\theta(\log t;\, z)\right)
    \;+\;
    \log \!\left(\frac{\partial h_\theta}{\partial \log t}(\log t;\, z)\right)
    \;-\;
    \log t,
    \label{eq:logpdf}\\[2pt]
    \log S_{T|Z}(t \mid z)
    &\;=\;
    \log \Phi\!\left(-\,h_\theta(\log t;\, z)\right),
    \label{eq:logsurv}
\end{align}
where $\phi$ and $\Phi$ are the standard-normal density and CDF. The Jacobian
$\partial h_\theta / \partial \log t$ has a simple closed form,
\[
    \frac{\partial h_\theta}{\partial \log t}(\log t;\, z)
    \;=\;
    a(z)
    \;+\;
    \sum_{k=1}^{K}
        \frac{\alpha_k(z)}{s_k(z)}
        \operatorname{sech}^{2}\!\left(\frac{\log t - \mu_k(z)}{s_k(z)}\right),
\]
so both~\eqref{eq:logpdf} and~\eqref{eq:logsurv} are exact, differentiable in
$\theta$, and numerically stable: we compute $\log \Phi(-h)$ via
\texttt{torch.special.ndtr} on $-h$ to avoid catastrophic cancellation in the
upper tail. We instantiate two independent flows $h_{\theta_1}, h_{\theta_2}$
for $T$ and $C$ respectively, with disjoint parameters.

\paragraph{Comparison with the parametric LogNormal alternative.}
The natural parametric counterpart of~\eqref{eq:flow} is the conditional
LogNormal, in which the transform reduces to
$h_{\text{LN}}(\log t; z) = (\log t - \mu(z)) / \sigma(z)$. This is the
$K=0$ special case and only spans the LogNormal family. The full flow with
$K \ge 1$ tanh components is strictly more expressive: it can represent
unimodal distributions whose hazard is non-monotone in $\log t$, including
Cox--PH-shaped hazards and heavy-tailed log-Student-$t$ shapes that are
provably outside the LogNormal family. Our simulation study
(Section~\ref{sec:sim}) quantifies the gap.

\subsection{Censored joint log-likelihood}
\label{sec:mle}

Under conditionally independent censoring, the contribution of a single
observation $(x, \delta, z)$ to the joint log-likelihood factorizes as
\begin{align}
    \ell_\theta(x, \delta, z)
    &\;=\;
    \delta \,\Big[\log f_{T|Z}(x \mid z) + \log S_{C|Z}(x \mid z)\Big]
    \nonumber\\
    &\quad
    +\;
    (1 - \delta)\,\Big[\log S_{T|Z}(x \mid z) + \log f_{C|Z}(x \mid z)\Big].
    \label{eq:censmle}
\end{align}
Both flows enter additively, so the four terms in~\eqref{eq:censmle} are
evaluated through~\eqref{eq:logpdf} and~\eqref{eq:logsurv} on the same forward
pass. The MLE objective is
\(
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{MLE}}(\theta_1, \theta_2)
    \;=\;
    -\,\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \ell_\theta(X_i, \delta_i, Z_i).
\)

\subsection{Soft--Nelson--Aalen Wasserstein regularizer}
\label{sec:reg}

The censored MLE in~\eqref{eq:censmle} is sample-level: it scores each
observation against its conditional density but does not directly constrain
the model-implied population survival curve to match the observed one. We
augment it with a population-level distributional constraint based on the
Nelson--Aalen estimator and its $1$-Wasserstein distance to a model-simulated
counterpart.

\paragraph{Observation-side anchor.}
Given $\mathcal{D}$, define the empirical Nelson--Aalen survival estimator on
a fixed time grid $\tau_0 < \tau_1 < \cdots < \tau_M$,
\[
    \widehat{S}_{\text{obs}}(\tau_m)
    \;=\;
    \exp\!\left(
        -\sum_{j \le m}
        \frac{
            \sum_{i} \mathbf{1}\{X_i \in [\tau_{j-1}, \tau_j)\}\, \delta_i
        }{
            \sum_{i} \mathbf{1}\{X_i \ge \tau_j\}
        }
    \right).
\]
This curve is held fixed during training.

\paragraph{Model-side simulation.}
For each batch we draw $S$ independent simulations of size $n$. For
$s = 1, \dots, S$ and $i = 1, \dots, n$ we sample
\(
    \tau_1^{(s,i)},\, \tau_2^{(s,i)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1),
\)
push them through the inverses of the two flows to obtain
$T_i^{(s)}, C_i^{(s)}$, and form the simulated censored pair
\(
    X_i^{(s)} = \min(T_i^{(s)}, C_i^{(s)}),
\)
\(
    \widetilde{\delta}_i^{(s)}
    = \sigma\!\left((C_i^{(s)} - T_i^{(s)}) / \tau_{\text{temp}}\right),
\)
where the soft event indicator uses a sigmoid with temperature
$\tau_{\text{temp}}$ to keep the eventual loss differentiable through
$\delta$. The corresponding \emph{differentiable} Nelson--Aalen survival on
the same grid is
\[
    \widetilde{S}_{\text{sim}}^{(s)}(\tau_m)
    \;=\;
    \exp\!\left(
        -\sum_{j \le m}
        \frac{
            \sum_i \widetilde{\delta}_i^{(s)} \, \kappa_\alpha(X_i^{(s)} - \tau_j)
        }{
            \sum_i \sigma\!\left((X_i^{(s)} - \tau_j) / \alpha\right)
            + \varepsilon
        }
    \right),
\]
where the soft at-risk count uses a sigmoid with temperature $\alpha$ and the
soft event count $\kappa_\alpha$ uses a difference-of-sigmoids window of the
same temperature.

\paragraph{Wasserstein objective.}
For two survival curves on the real line, the $1$-Wasserstein distance equals
the absolute area between them,
\(
    W_1\!\left(\widehat{S}_{\text{obs}},\, \widetilde{S}_{\text{sim}}^{(s)}\right)
    =
    \int_0^{\infty}
    \big|
        \widehat{S}_{\text{obs}}(t) - \widetilde{S}_{\text{sim}}^{(s)}(t)
    \big|\,
    \mathrm{d}t,
\)
which we discretize by the trapezoidal rule on the grid $\{\tau_m\}$.
Averaging across the $S$ simulations gives the regularizer
\[
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{NA-W}}(\theta_1, \theta_2)
    \;=\;
    \frac{1}{S} \sum_{s=1}^{S}
        W_1\!\left(\widehat{S}_{\text{obs}},\, \widetilde{S}_{\text{sim}}^{(s)}\right).
\]
The total training objective is
\[
    \mathcal{L}(\theta_1, \theta_2)
    \;=\;
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{MLE}}(\theta_1, \theta_2)
    \;+\;
    \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{NA-W}}(\theta_1, \theta_2),
\]
with regularization strength $\lambda > 0$ tuned on a held-out split.

\subsection{Differentiable sampling via implicit reparameterization}
\label{sec:samp}

Computing $\mathcal{L}_{\text{NA-W}}$ requires backpropagating through samples
$T_i^{(s)} = h_{\theta_1}^{-1}(\tau_1^{(s,i)};\, Z_i)$ and analogously for $C$.
Because the inverse of~\eqref{eq:flow} has no closed form, we evaluate it by
$40$ steps of vectorized bisection on $\log t \in [\log t_{\min},\, \log t_{\max}]$
inside a \texttt{torch.no\_grad} block, returning a numerically accurate
$\log t^{*}$. The bisection has zero gradient with respect to $\theta$, so a
naive implementation would block backpropagation through the regularizer
entirely. We instead reattach the gradient via the implicit-function
construction of \citet{figurnov2018implicit}: define
\begin{equation}
    \log t^{\diamond}
    \;=\;
    \operatorname{stop\_grad}(\log t^{*})
    \;-\;
    \frac{
        h_\theta\!\left(\log t^{*};\, z\right) - u
    }{
        \operatorname{stop\_grad}\!\left(\partial h_\theta / \partial \log t\,(\log t^{*};\, z)\right)
    }.
    \label{eq:implicit}
\end{equation}
Numerically $\log t^{\diamond} = \log t^{*}$ to floating-point precision, but
its derivative with respect to $\theta$ equals the exact implicit gradient
$-(\partial h_\theta/\partial \theta) / (\partial h_\theta/\partial \log t)$
evaluated at $\log t^{*}$. Substituting $T = \exp(\log t^{\diamond})$ in the
regularizer therefore yields exact gradients through the inverse without
solving any system of equations and without unrolling the bisection. The same
construction is used for $C$.

\paragraph{Direction of computation.}
We emphasize that the training-time \emph{forward} direction in this
construction is $\log t \mapsto u$ (Equation~\eqref{eq:flow}), so the
likelihood
in~\eqref{eq:logpdf}--\eqref{eq:logsurv} is closed-form and never requires the
inverse: the likelihood needs only $h_\theta$ and its Jacobian, both of which
are explicit. The inverse is invoked exclusively for sampling, where the
implicit-reparameterization trick~\eqref{eq:implicit} provides exact
gradients. This is in contrast to flow constructions that train through the
inverse with bisection, in which the derivative of the inverse must be
recovered, often incorrectly, by automatic differentiation through the
bisection itself.

\subsection{Implementation details}
\label{sec:impl}

The MLP feeding the flow heads has two hidden layers of width $32$ with
$\tanh$ nonlinearities, and the flow uses $K=4$ tanh components. We train
with Adam at learning rate $10^{-3}$, gradient clipping at $\ell_2$-norm $5$,
batch size equal to the training-set size, regularizer weight $\lambda = 0.2$,
$S=3$ Monte~Carlo simulations per batch, soft temperatures
$\tau_{\text{temp}} = 0.25$ and $\alpha = 0.20$, and a Nelson--Aalen grid of
$M = 80$ points spanning the empirical $1$\textsuperscript{st}--$99$\textsuperscript{th}
percentile of the observed $X$. Time is clipped to
$[10^{-4},\, 10]$ for both true and simulated samples. We early-stop on the
training objective with patience $35$ and a relative tolerance of $10^{-4}$.
A reference implementation is provided in the supplementary material.
